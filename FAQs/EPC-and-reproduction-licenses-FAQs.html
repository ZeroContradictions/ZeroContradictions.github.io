7. Why do you want to control reproduction?

The social control of reproduction is not a new idea. It is a very old and almost universal practice. In the past, reproduction was controlled by the institution of marriage. Almost every society had some form of marriage that regulated human sexual behavior. Sex outside marriage was not allowed. We abandoned the traditional control of reproduction without understanding its function or the consequences of abandoning it. We are now seeing the consequences (and paying the costs) of abandoning marriage. It is time to replace marriage with a modern form of reproduction control.

8. How would you enforce an EPC system with reproduction licenses?

There are various ways to do it. A person who violates the policy could be sterilized and/or forced to be on birth control until they can repay society for the incurred costs of supporting the child. Supervised birth control and regular testing should be required for people on welfare and convicted criminals.

9. Wouldn’t EPC be horribly authoritarian?

No. It would be no more authoritarian than limiting the freedom to drive or the freedom to kill other people. Reproduction has serious consequences that can be harmful to others, and so the imposition of social constraints on reproduction is justified for the same reason that social constraints on other behaviors are justified.

10. Wouldn’t EPC be difficult to enforce?

No. It would be easier than enforcing many current laws, including traffic regulations and the law against murder. The law against murder is difficult to enforce because it is often difficult to identify murderers. Some murders go unsolved. No one uses this as an argument that murder should be legal. It would be much easier to identify people who have unlicensed children than it currently is to identify murderers. It’s pretty hard to conceal a child.

11. Wouldn’t Eugenics “interfere with the natural order of things”?

That’s a silly objection (and the naturalistic fallacy). What is the natural order of things? Until recently, most children died young. Now, with modern civilization (based on fossil fuels) we allow most children to live to adulthood. If eugenics violates the natural order then so does modern civilization. And without eugenics modern civilization is unsustainable. Eugenic population control is needed to balance the effects of modern prosperity on the human population. We could use eugenic population control to create a new “natural order” of sustainable prosperity. Why shouldn’t we? Nature will not strike us down for using eugenics. In fact, the opposite is true. Nature will eventually destroy our civilization if we don’t regulate our population eugenically.

12. Didn’t the Nazis Try to Implement Eugenics?

12.1. Why The Nazis Actually Failed To Implement Eugenics On A Scientific/Rational Basis

From a scientific perspective, the Nazis didn’t advocate for policies that enforced positive eugenics. There is absolutely no scientific evidence that blonde hair, blue eyes, white skin, and other “Aryan” physical characteristics lead to higher intelligence. Insisting otherwise is pseudo-scientific and racist.

For someone who purportedly promoted positive eugenics, Hitler only did that in name only, and in fact accomplished the exact opposite of that. That’s what the Nazis said they were doing, but what were they actually doing? Instead of breeding people who were scientifically proven to be more intelligent, physically-fit, etc than the average human, he killed millions of people from the most successful ethnic group in history: the Jews. Given the Jews’ strong record of making scientific accomplishments, contributing to human progress, and acquiring positions of power, killing millions of them is the opposite of what someone should be doing if they want to promote positive eugenic traits like intelligence or physical fitness.

Instead, the Nazis promoted racist, pseudo-scientific garbage, and used that as an excuse for killing some of the most productive members of society. I would like to very strongly emphasize to the fullest extent possible that Racism is NOT eugenics.

However, it can be clearly argued that the Nazis did promote negative eugenics, to the extent that that they knew how to. Preventing physically disabled, mentally disabled, diseased, etc people from reproducing is undeniably a form of negative eugenics, which they did enforce, so it would be erroneous to say that the Nazis didn’t enforce scientifically-based eugenics at all. They did some negative eugenics, but they also did the opposite of what positive eugenics should be like.

Hitler supported racism, mass murder, and pseudo-science. What I am advocating for is REAL eugenics, i.e. policies that are backed by reasoning, scientific evidence, and non-partisanship.

15. Wouldn’t eugenics decrease genetic diversity?

Yes in the sense that it would definitely decrease the frequency of genes that cause people to be dumber, more violent, less productive, etc, but that’s exactly what we want. How could there possibly be any downsides to removing them from the gene pool? And what benefits would there be to keeping those genes within the gene pool?

Aside from that, under the solution that we’re proposing, the goal of eugenics wouldn’t be to increase or decrease genetic diversity. Contrary to this question, eugenics could actually increase genetic diversity if there were desirable rare genes that got selected for.

15.1. Eugenics As A Means To Increase Genetic Diversity, Rather Than To Reduce It

So the strongest argument against eugenics might be that it reduces genetic diversity, but what if a eugenics policy merely only aims to alter the proportions to which certain genes and alleles exist within the population, not to completely eliminate one gene in favor of another? For instance, if two hypothetical genes A and B exist in a population at percentages 80% and 20% respectively, what if the goal was to cause those genes to exist at 50% each? As another example, it is known that most humans have trichromatic vision and that tetrachromatic vision only exists in very, very few humans, perhaps only women exclusively. If a eugenics or genetic engineering policy aimed to increase the percentage of people with tetrachromatic vision in the population, then this would be another example of eugenics leading to an increase in genetic diversity, and one that seems like it should be very favorable.

Going even further, if the goal of a eugenics policy was to increase genetic diversity rather than to decrease it, this would lead to some very interesting things to ponder about. For one thing, the strongest argument against eugenics that it would decrease genetic diversity would not apply.

Alternatively, a eugenics policy might aim to modify the genetic composition to be more uneven, like modifying genes A and B to exist from 50% each to percentage ratios like 70/30, 80/20, or even 90/10, but not to 100/0% where one of the genes would be completely eliminated from the population.

It could potentially be very beneficial for society to breed people with type-O blood (the universal blood donor) that have a strong sense for altruism or wanting to help people that they are very likely to donate lots and lots of blood to ensure a stable supply for society.

For that matter, the same could be even more easily said for the people who possess that extremely rare blood type that is even more beneficial than type-O blood. In Australia, there is someone whose donated blood has been used to develop some medication that has saved the lives of an estimated ~2 million people.
