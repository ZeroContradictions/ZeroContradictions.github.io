2. What is Overpopulation?

Overpopulation is when there is an excessive number of occupants (people, animals, plants, etc.) in a particular area; specifically, when the number of occupants exceeds the ability of that area to provide for them. Overpopulation is preceded by ecological overshoot.

The primary misconception of overpopulation is that it only depends on the number of organisms/people. This view assumes that the only factor that matters for overpopulation is the number of organisms within the environment. It further assumes that, if a certain number of individuals can be supported in the present, there’ll be no problems in the future, which I’ve debunked here.

This view is wrong and very ignorant about biology. Overpopulation is determined by at least four parameters:

  • Number of creatures (usually of a single species) in an area
  • An area and its resources
  • The average ecological footprint per creature (for humans, this is the average standard of living per capita)
  • A timeframe
  • (for humans) The technology and substitution options available to those creatures
  • (for humans) The distribution of wealth and opportunities (which is heavily influenced by the system of property norms).

To quote from “Malthus Being Wrong Doesn’t Make You Right” on justopia.org:

1,000 people living in the fertile crescent isn’t overpopulation. 1,000 people living on a 2-acre island would almost certainly be so. An area can support far fewer people living first world lifestyles (50-100 full-time energy slaves) than ones living like the typical African bushman.

Overpopulation also considers the amount of time a population can be sustained, though one could argue this ties into “the resources in an area” bullet point. Right now, humans are drawing down oil reserves which have taken millions of years to form. If we’re only considering the resources humanity has, 8 billion people can probably be sustained until the energy returned on energy invested from oil becomes nearly 1:1. There may be enough time for humanity to switch to different energy sources like nuclear energy, but it’s not guaranteed…

The concept of overshoot requires a time dimension. Given widely known technology, humans are in overshoot now. That means that without finding a new energy source or drastically lowering the ecological footprint per person (AKA having a shittier lifestyle), there is simply not going to be 8 billion humans alive simultaneously within 100 years: the current number of humans on Earth is simply not sustainable. ― Justin D Keith

3. Hasn’t Malthusianism been debunked?

Thomas Malthus is widely regarded as the most famous early intellectual of population dynamics. In 1798, Malthus wrote “An Essay on the Principles of Population”, stating:

“Must it not then be acknowledged by an attentive examiner of the histories of mankind, that in every age and in every state in which man has existed, or does now exist:

That the increase of population is necessarily limited by the means of subsistence.

That population does invariably increase when the means of subsistence increase.

And that the superior power of population it repressed, and the actual population kept equal to the means of subsistence, by misery and vice?“

Malthus was correct that human populations had historically been limited by subsistence farming (at the time he wrote the essay), but he was wrong that this would always be the case in the future because he didn’t correctly anticipate the discovery of additional energy sources, and didn’t see the contributions people like Norman Borlaug made through genetically engineering crops. So Malthus’ thesis about linear production versus exponential consumption rates was incorrect. However, what Malthus’s claim in the essay is different from the definition of Malthusianism.

Malthusianism: The theory that potentially exponential population growth can surpass the linear growth of an environment’s carrying capacity (including its food supply and other resources), which eventually causes members of the population to die off until settling back down to the carrying capacity.

Neo-Malthusianism: The theory and concern that overpopulation (and overconsumption) may increase resource depletion and/or environmental degradation and lead to ecological collapse or other hazards. Neo-Malthusianism is often coupled with advocacy of human population planning to ensure sufficient resources for current and future human populations as well as for other species.

We are not defending what Malthus wrote in 1798 (that populations are limited by subsistence farming). Instead, we are arguing in favor of Malthusianism, which is a completely different concept that can be defended by an upgraded knowledge of history, population dynamics, and evolutionary reasoning. It does not follow that Malthus being wrong means that the concepts of overpopulation and ecological overshoot are incorrect, not applicable to humans, or not a risk which bears investigation. We support well-grounded Neo-Malthusian solutions as a solution to the overpopulation disaster that is bound to plague humanity within the 21st century.

6.1. Overpopulation is a Free-Rider Problem

The problem with assuming that overpopulation can be solved by most people choosing to have fewer children is that many people will choose not to have less children. The people who choose to have the highest fertility levels while everybody else has a below-replacement fertility thus create a free-rider problem. They will benefit from having a higher reproductive success, their children will benefit from having more resources available for them to exploit since everybody else chose to have fewer children, and everybody else will be negatively affected because they had lower reproductive success and fewer resources available for them since the high-fertility people left behind fewer resources for everybody else. For more info, see: every existing organism can only exist at the cost of another.

The human population will not be limited by voluntary low fertility, because evolution will eliminate the memes and genes associated with voluntarily low fertility. In an environment of abundance, in which most offspring live to adulthood, high fertility is selected for. Even if most people choose low fertility today, by doing so they select against the traits that make people choose low fertility. The future is determined by who shows up. Human nature (like the nature of every other kind of life) is determined by who reproduces. Those who reproduce pass their traits on. Modern abundance has not taken the struggle out of life. It has just changed the nature of the struggle, from a struggle over resources to a breeding contest. Right now, whoever has the most kids wins.

It’s true that most people in developed societies choose to have few or no children. That doesn’t mean that most people in the future will have low fertility. Why? Because people in the future have to be born, and more of them will be born to people with high fertility than low fertility. Even if almost everyone chooses low fertility, those with higher fertility will simply outbreed them in a few generations. For example, the Amish alone, could replace the entire world population in less than 300 years, at their current rate of growth.

11. How is Population Control Related to Eugenics?

When a population reaches the carrying capacity of its environment, competition and eugenic selection occur in order to select the organisms that are the most reproductively successful in the scarce resources environment.

By removing the selectionary pressures on humanity and limitations that have traditionally limited the carrying capacity of the human population, not only does this cause dysgenics from the removal of said selectionary pressures, but it also causes overpopulation. That said, if we achieved legally-enforced population control for humanity, then we would be placing artificial selectionary pressures that will increase the eugenic quality of the human population, that would otherwise never occur.

If someone is going to be in favor of population control, ought to be in favor of bottom-up eugenics as well (top-down eugenics would fail the same reason why command economies fail). Many people today are against eugenics because it would require restricting some people from having children, but population control does the same thing and population control will be necessary in order to prevent catastrophic disasters from happening in the future, so this is not a good argument against eugenics.

18. Why do we need to worry about overpopulation when technology and freer markets could save us instead?

Cornucopians greatly overestimate the power of technology.

For one thing, technology has had many negative effects on society, and in many cases, it backfired by making society arguably worse.

To whatever extent that technological advancement has temporarily raised the Earth’s carrying capacity, this ignores that technology has also been fueling a demand for higher standards of living all around the world, which is more taxing on the environment and thus increases the risk for overpopulation.

One of the problems with assuming that technology will solve overpopulation is that industrial technologically advanced societies require large populations in order to sustain themselves, lest there will be no specialization and no division of labor.

Relying on technology to always raise the society’s carrying capacity is not a viable solution because populations naturally reproduce to infinity, and the only thing holding them back is the carrying capacity and the natural selection that results from that. Right now, the primary selectionary pressure on the human genome is birth control, which selects people who consciously choose to have children. Once the human population adapts to resist the limiting effects that birth control has on the human population, an unstoppable population explosion will happen, unless breaching the carrying capacity causes war, disease, and famine.

23. What would the world population be if the black plague never happened?

I once had a history teacher who estimated that that the current population of Europe would be 10 times greater if the Black Plague never happened, but this uses fallacious reasoning. There’s no way that could ever happen anyway since that would require increasing the carrying capacity of Europe by ten-fold as a result of the black plague never happening.

Yes, the Black Plague did reduce the European population by about a third, but since biological populations are selected to reproduce up to the carrying capacity, it’s more likely that the black plague only temporarily widened the gap between the current population and its maximum carrying capacity. Since there were temporarily more resources per capita available to all the living humans due to the massive decline in population, this means that the surviving humans were likely able to have more children than previous generations. Thus, the European population would’ve returned to the carrying capacity irregardless of whether the plague ever happened.

24. What will Humanity’s Next Overpopulation Crisis look like?

The next major overpopulation crisis will probably happen when a catastrophic event causes the carrying capacity to suddenly and unexpectedly shrink, rather than the population surpassing a stagnant carrying capacity. If this happens, it’ll probably less likely to be caused by a blight or disease in the food supply since modern agricultural techniques probably use more genetically diverse crops, but we still cannot underestimate the potential for such massive catastrophes. Instead, the next major event that could cause a worldwide overpopulation crisis is more likely to be a massive war (which restricts the food supply for much of the world via embargoes, blockades, destruction of food production).

The people of today who are not concerned with overpopulation and ridicule Malthus for being wrong (so far) tend to believe that overpopulation would look like the Earth’s population and the Earth’s resources both increasing (the latter due to technological advancements), with Earth’s population increasing faster. Not only does this ignore the possibility that a major/unprecendented catastrophe could cause overpopulation to happen instantly, but it’s also less likely than the catastrophe-caused scenario since a severe catastrophe could happen at any moment.

All it will take to prove that Neo-Malthusianism concerns are/were warranted is for an unprecendented emergency to happen. For example, suppose the following series of plausible geopolitical events occurs:

  1. China invades Taiwan,
  2. The US military defends Taiwan,
  3. India takes the opportunity to attack China and seize Tibet while China is distracted,
  4. Japan and South Korea support Taiwan’s defense as well in order to protect their international trading routes through the Strait of Taiwan,
  5. North Korea might takes the opportunity to attack South Korea while they are distracted,
  6. Every country around the world picks a side (China vs the US),
  7. WWIII starts,
  8. massive food shortages begin everywhere due to the disruption of free trade, and
  9. the world faces unprecedented overpopulation due to all of the aforementioned.

This is a very realistic scenario, especially amid the recent rapid growth of the Chinese military and increasing Chinese aggression around Taiwan. The Russian invasion of Ukraine shouldn’t leave the world doubting that China could follow suit and invade Taiwan whenever it chooses. If this or a similarly catastrophic scenario occurs, Neo-Malthusianism will be proven right eventually. Once again, History shows that overpopulation can happen unpredictably within mere months, so anyone would be incredibly naive to think that it’s never something to worry about.
