2. The Libertarian NAP Is Vague And Misleading

Although the Libertarian NAP is defined as an ethical rule prohibiting all actions that violate negative rights, there is disagreement among Libertarians regarding what should and should not count as negative rights. Libertarians might be inclined to believe that it’s obvious as to what counts as a negative right since it can be asked as a yes/no question, but it’s actually not that simple because there are lots of gray areas. And even if you try to resolve the gray areas by definitively drawing a line in the sand, this is not going to work for everybody. Some people are going to disagree with the ultimate decision, whether that’s a small minority, or whether the disagreement is split half and half among the population. For example, two Libertarians might say that they both believe in the NAP, but if you dig deeper and ask more specific questions, they actually disagree on a number of things (abortion, IP rights, rainwater rights, etc). When I used to be a Libertarian, this I used to believe this (that it is obvious regarding what does and does not count as negative rights) until I started asking deeper questions about ethics (e.g. Georgism) and became a moral relativist.

The Non-Aggression Principle (NAP) is incompatible with human nature, although it is notable for being a good first attempt at trying to establish the rules of a cooperative society.

It is misleading that Libertarians try to conceptualize the NAP as a standard for objective morality. In order for this to be the case, there would have to be an objective definition of what does and does not count as “aggression”. But since “aggression” is subjective, the NAP is also subjective. That also means that there are at least dozens or hundreds of different conceptions of the NAP, instead of just one.

Morality is not objective because morality necessarily has to depend on perspective. Moreover, it is also very dangerous to view morality as completely black and white.

3.4. Voluntary Self-Protection Versus The Free-Rider Problem

Ancaps frequently argue that people won’t need the protection of a state as long as people are free to form mutual protection agreements. Unless there is a top-down authority that will ensure that everybody will protect the community when called upon to do so, there will be a free-rider problem where the members of the voluntary army are incentivized to not protect the community so that they don’t have to put their life on the line, while they still get the benefits of everybody else doing the actual fighting and defending. The end result will be that the voluntary army only consists of ghost soldiers. The same argument also applies to conscription if there arises a situation where conscription is necessary to defend the country. It’s truly puzzling how Ancaps are quick to realize that welfare states are doomed to fail since they create a free-rider problem, and yet their unable to realize that their own proposals are also doomed to fail due to free-rider problems.

On the other hand, the ancap army could have an authority that will ensure that every person on the military rolls is guaranteed to fight if the community is under attack. In order to do this, it would have to punish defectors, and that would avoid the free-rider problem. But in that case, it wouldn’t be a voluntary army anymore. It would just be another state.

If you’re going to break the IMSA, then society is justified to prosecute you.

9. The “Free” Markets in Ancapistan Are Established By Circular-Reasoning

Government is necessary in order to establish, guarantee, and protect a free market, lest the market be neither free nor fair.

The basic error with anarchism is that it evades the crucial, life-and-death difference between trade and force. Objectivism recognizes that because man is the rational animal, he can only succeed if he is free of coercion. If men are allowed to trade freely, he will prosper and succeed. If men are subject to coercion (practiced either by the state or by criminals) he will fail and die. Anarchists conflate these two - they make no distinction between trade and force and simply argue that force is just a kind of trade like any other so it should be on the market.

They argue that the roles that would be traditionally filled by government - police, courts and army - should simply be filled by a free market. But this is plainly circular reasoning. The protection of individual rights cannot be supplied by a market since the very existence of a market itself requires the protection of individual rights. You get into an infinite regress trying to explain this away.

Furthermore the notion that the state has an immoral monopoly on coercion is a false comparison. Competition in coercion is nothing like competition in a marketplace. Competing in a marketplace means you have several goods to choose from and there is no coercion at all. Competing in coercion would mean… what exactly? All force is a monopoly - force says obey or harm will be done to you. The notion of “competition” in force is silly, unless you mean five independent thugs pointing guns at you with different demands.

Another key reason why a limited government is necessary is to place the awesome power of retaliatory force under objective laws. With a limited government, specific rules can be placed on its functioning which will limit the government’s power and ensure it adheres to certain basic rules. Under anarcho-capitalism, any private agency can do whatever it wants - if a group wants to have the death penalty for adultery, that is perfectly OK. If another group thinks thermonuclear warfare is a proper response to property dispute, who is to stop them - after all, we wouldn’t want to interfere with their “right” to use retaliatory force. Limited government provides the possibility of placing the means of coercion under objective control - anarcho-capitalism lets anybody do absolutely anything no matter how wicked, unjust or disproportionate.

And the final reason is the very fact that anarchy simply cannot exist as you envision it. The only valid meaning of the term anarchy is the way it was historically used - to mean a situation where no central authority results in constant violence and bloodshed between different, feuding mini-dictatorships (i.e. Somalia). The idea of anarchy as a society where everyone magically lives in harmony without the use of force is a fantasy - nothing like this has ever existed in history on any scale whatever, the reason being that such a society would fall victim to the first thug or enemy army who showed up (if they could resist those two, then they do have some force-wielding entity which is then a de-facto government).

So, in short, anarcho-“capitalism” conflates trade and force, rests on circular logic, embraces complete subjectivism and is logically incoherent. Anarchy means Somalia, Afghanistan and Iraq - If it is freedom you want, you must support the existence of a government which will guarantee it.

9.1. Ancaps’ Circular Reasoning Stated In Lines Of Logical Propositions

[A] Individual rights will be protected exclusively by private entities, without any government protection.

[B] Private defense corporations will compete against each other in a free marketplace with the intent of gaining the most profit.

[C] A free and fair marketplace can only exist if individual rights (trade, property, etc) are protected, otherwise the marketplace is not free nor fair.

If you believe all the premises above as well as: (A -> B) and (B -> C) and (C -> B), then you believe in a circular reasoning fallacy. It would not be possible to sufficiently protect individual rights without a free marketplace that could distribute the necessary weapons and defenses, private defense corporations would not exist if the free marketplace does not exist, and the free marketplace does not exist because there is no entity that can independently guarantee its existence since we already assumed premise A. By negation introduction, we can reject proposition A.

9.2. Refuting Ancap Objections

Ancaps might counter-argue that communities could still provide their own defenses until a free market forms, but this is also impossible because no community or defense agency would be able to purchase the goods and services that would be necessary for protecting themselves against foreign invaders (guns, ammunition, defenses), which would effectively means that everybody would be defenseless unless they already had modern weapons and defenses to begin with. Producing modern weapons on an industrial scale is essentially impossible without an existing free market that could enable the production and distribution of defensive goods, and no free market can exist since individual rights cannot be protected without sufficient defenses, and moreover, because there are no governments that can guarantee a market in the first place.

And even if communities were able to provide their own private, mutual protection, they would still be vulnerable to collapsing due to the Free-Rider Problem.

If Community X has a greater Comparative Advantage and a greater Division of Labor at producing better quality weapons than Community Y, then Community X would be able to subdue Community Y by force if they wanted to. But if governments existed, they could prevent Community X from conquering Community Y, and it would be possible to establish a free marketplace that could produce and distribute high-quality weapons across all regions.

Conclusion: A laissez-faire government is necessary to protect a free market where corporations of any kind are able to compete against each other.

11.1. Defining Rothbardian Anarcho-Capitalism Versus Private Cities Anarcho-Capitalism

Rothbardian Anarcho-Capitalism (RAC): Anarcho-Capitalism as it was first conceived by Murray Rothbard. Under RAC, multiple political corporations can have overlapping authority on the same plot of territory.

Private Cities Anarcho-Capitalism (PCAC): Anarcho-Capitalism, except that only one political corporation can have control over a plot of territory. Different communities that favor different laws, legal codes, customs, and such would each form their own private city where their ideals are exclusively enforced.

When I went through my Ancap phase, I started off as a Rothbardian Anarcho-Capitalist, but then I became a Private Cities Anarcho-Capitalist as I found it harder and harder to reconcile the problems with Rothbardian Anarcho-Capitalism, until I eventually rejected Anarcho-Capitalism altogether. Most people don’t make a distinction between these two types of Anarcho-Capitalism, but I argue that this is an important distinction to make because I’ve found some Private City Anarcho-Capitalists don’t like RAC, and understandably so, because PCAC has far fewer problems than RAC and is more likely to be successful, hence why I converted from the former to the latter near the end of my Ancap phase.

12. Advancing Technology Isn’t Necessarily A Good Thing

In this section, I write my response to some naive quotes that have been written by ancaps who don’t realize that technology is a double-edged sword.

You wanna know what else are great uses for bitcoin and crypto? Scams, ransom, hacking attempts, human trafficking, et cetera.
