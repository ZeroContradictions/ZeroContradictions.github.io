1. Thinking About Dystopias

If people live in a dystopia, but they don’t recognize how or why it is oppressive, is it still a dystopia?

  • I would be so disappointed if I had the resources to figure out that I live in a dystopia, but I never did before I died.
    • The only effective remedy to thoroughly question everything and always keep an open mind.
    • Unfortunately it is so easy for close-minded people to believe they are open-minded, which is one of main reasons why most people are unaware there is dystopia.
  • Consider the DNA delusion. If people are programmed to believe that the world is not a dystopia, is it still a dystopia?
    • This thought experiment gets interesting. It implies that whether or not a society can be deemed a utopia/dystopia/neither depends on the perception of sentient organisms.
    • Because no utopias nor dystopias could ever exist if a society of living organisms didn’t exist.
      • For that matter, epistemology, ethics, all the other branches of philosophy (except metaphysics), and all human-centric academic fields wouldn’t exist either.
    • Going even further, if there was only one sentient organism, but no society of sentient organisms, their perception of their own life still seems relevant to the utopia/dystopia question, even if there is no society present.
    • Although I’ve only been talking about sentient organisms, I’m pretty sure that the designation of a society as utopia/dystopia/neither is independent of whether or not the organisms have the conscious free will to make a determination on society’s status.

Conclusion: Societal perception is the key to determining whether a society is a utopia/dystopia/neither.

  • However, determining the label that a society should receive gets very murky and unclear when there is any number of members of the society that disagree with how the majority perceives the society.
  • Maybe a distinction should be made to consider “perceived utopia/dystopia/neither” versus “actual utopia/dystopia/neither”?
  • I would be equally disappointed if I was wrong on something critical, but I didn’t figure it out before I died.

If everybody lives in a dystopia, but no sentient organism is able to realize it, and/or thinks that the dystopia is worth living through, is it still a bad thing? If evolutionary DNA creates all of the sentient world, all sentient life has a net-negative life experience, but still continues forward and wants to continue creating more DNA-programmed life, would this still be a dystopia even if it isn’t perceived as one? Even if all the sentient life thought the dystopia was worth living through and couldn’t identify anything wrong with the society itself, the best argument that it is still a dystopia is that bad things would still be continuing to happen. Bad things would be happening frequently and although nobody likes them and nobody is able to identify the society/system itself as being the problem, it would still be a dystopia. The source of all the problems is still there, nobody likes the problems, and yet nobody is able to identify the source of all the problems, even though they are programmed to like the society and not see anything wrong with the system itself. The sentient life would be conflicted between its likeness for the existing system and all the problems that the system causes.

Definitions/Conceptions Of Freedom Various Philosophical Definitions Of Freedom Georgist Definition Of Freedom: I Classical Liberal Definition Of Freedom: I Voluntaryist Definition Of Freedom: I

  • The power to do as one wants without hindrance, restraint, or coercion from a third party, so long as they abide by the Non-Aggression Principle.
  • Individuals, businesses, charities, educational institutions, religious institutions, homeowner’s associations, and other organizations where membership is 100% voluntary can have freedom.
  • Dead people cannot be free because they don’t have free will, however, if someone wrote a will letter before they died (or anything equivalent to such a thing), then that letter carries the full force of their free will for when they were alive, because they wrote it when they were still alive.
  • Voluntary: Anything done with free will, without coercion. Even if someone was intoxicated or manipulated into doing something, that still counts as free will because they could have chosen to not do it instead. If someone is asleep or unconscious, then they do not have free will at the moment.
  • Coercion: The practice of using force or threats to persuade someone to do something.
  • Force: Theft, murder, rape, fraud, damage, or threats.
  • If I say that children do not have free will, then I will inevitably encounter the problem defining the age when someone does have free will. I know this is going to be extremely arbitrary, but I will propose that anybody who is at least 13 years old, or has sexual urges that they want to act on, has free will.
  • And even if someone doesn’t have free will, they still have negative rights, which are important, but not the same thing as freedom.
