1. The Constitutional Principles Of The Government

How to govern society is one of the oldest philosophical problems, and we don’t have any ideal solution, just some pragmatic and heuristic ones.

1.1. The Practicality of Constitutions

The de facto power of the United States Constitution is weaker than the de jure power of the Constitution, but this isn’t really surprising. At the end of the day, a Constitution is just a piece of paper created by the government to limit what the government can do. So if the government had the power to create the constitution and enforce it, then it can just as easily have the power to stop enforcing parts of it or even ignore it completely.

But if government is going to exist, it definitely wouldn’t be unreasonable to have a document of rules describing what the government must aim to do, as well as what it can and cannot do. Even most anarchists have implicit rules for their societies, and they would have to have a document that explicitly states the rules of their “societies” if they were actually serious about their ideas. That said, it’s still worth creating a Constitution that states the explicit rules of society and figuring out the best characteristics that a Constitution should have.

Conclusion: In order for a Constitution to have the true power and enforcement that its authors intended it to have, Constitutions must be enforced directly by the people instead of the government. It cannot be stressed enough that the citizens much be vigilant to veto, rebel, and reform if the legal system ever proposes an anti-constitutional update to the legal code, lest a deep state will form. However, I’m not entirely sure how such a society could be structured to make this more likely or even guaranteed to happen, if it’s even possible.

1.2. The Goals Of The Government

The first and foremost goal of government is to resolve Prisoner Dilemmas and Tragedies of the Commons. Government comes from the necessity to create a reasonable set of laws that would create a societal framework from which selfish individuals can cooperate together for greater mutual gain (than if they didn’t cooperate together). Cooperation is the foundation of civilization, and the best legal code is the one that enables that to the greatest extent possible.

Instead of calling the relationship between a citizen and the government a “social contract”, it shall instead be called an Implicit Mutual Social Agreement (IMSA). Every time the word “contract” is used, Libertarians and Ancaps complain that it’s not really a contract because they never signed it. And while they’re right about that, they’re missing the point. The idea is that if you want the benefits that society gives you, then you have to comply with society’s rules, even if you don’t like all the rules. You can’t have your cake and eat it too. If you’re going to break the IMSA, then society is justified to prosecute you.

1.3. Ensuring Accountability To The People

A politician can do what’s best for their constituents, but that won’t necessarily be what’s best for the entire world as a whole.

For example, a local lawmaker/governor might criticize a local company for outsourcing labor since that would take jobs away from their constituents (particularly in the short-term).

However, this is actually what is best for the entire world as a whole. The company wins because it reduces its labor costs, the newly hired laborers overseas win because they acquire higher-paying jobs, and in the long-term, the community that initially lost jobs will win too since they can purchase cheaper goods and services.

So even though the local politician might strive to prevent the company from outsourcing labor overseas, this action actually isn’t best for the world as a whole.

This is one more reason why government often works against the people, and unfortunately it happens very often. A more efficient government/reason-system ought to not do this.

1.4. Public Trust In The Government

[this section is still in development]

When people say “Never trust the government” or ask “Do you trust the government?”, what do either of those even mean? Government is very complex, there’s local, state, and federal levels, different branches of government, different agencies and departments of government, and many many different people who all work for the government.

When conservatives say “Never trust the government”, they will still typically trust things like the FDA, the USPS, NASA, local utilities, other government agencies, and probably many favored politicians. When anarchists or libertarians say the tagline, they typically have far less trust and approval for the government as a whole (as it currently exists), but even they will probably still count on at least a few functions of government to do things that they depend on.

So what specifically about the government should not be trusted? Any particular departments, agencies, or people filling administrative roles? And generally speaking, what aspects of government can be trusted?

Also, how does one define “trust”? Certainly there are different levels of trust, so what levels of trust can be applied to each component of the government? Are there different types of trust, or are they all the same thing?

1.6. Geopolitical Stability

Any proposal must be geopolitically stable. A network of city states would fail since it wouldn’t be geopolitically stable, so it is not viable.

There are lots of ideas that would not be geopolitically stable, and yet people think they’re immoral anyway. I couldn’t care less if people think that measures that ensure geopolitical stability are “immoral” (e.g. the selective service system, not allowing secession, maintaining a strong global military presence, etc). If the alternative is geopolitically instability, then those moralistic ideas are not worth any consideration because they would enable war, conflict, and the country’s downfall.()

Private cities are similar in concept to the nation-states that exist today, except that nation-states are bigger, authoritarian by default, and are characterized by local geography, socio-ethnolinguistic groups, history, cultures, etc. If one doesn’t like the country that they are currently living in, they (usually) have a choice to leave and migrate to another.

Paragraph #187: On the more sophisticated level the ideology should address itself to people who are intelligent, thoughtful and rational. The object should be to create a core of people who will be opposed to the industrial system on a rational, thought-out basis, with full appreciation of the problems and ambiguities involved, and of the price that has to be paid for getting rid of the system. 2.1.3. Creating A Veto System

  • When many liberal democracies offer voters yes/no propositions to vote for on a city, state, or national level, this is rather similar to the 2/3 direct democratic veto that I am proposing as a check on the reason system.
  • Among eligible participating voters, if for every person who does not disapprove of the legislation, there are two people who disapprove, then the legislation is vetoed. Although a skeptic might argue that percentages are arbitrary, two-thirds (or approximately 66.66%) is the lowest majority ratio that is higher than 50% (or half of the population) AND only uses integers.
  • Maybe it could even be a two to one ratio among eligible voters, regardless of whether everybody voted or not? Would this be practical at all?
    • Other integer ratios include three-fourths, four-fifths, five-sixths, etc. Maybe if a high enough ratio is reached, the members of the reason system decision council must be purged and re-elected?
  • Hopefully the system for direct democratic vetoes against the reason/legal system would not devolve to have people joining political organizations akin to the political parties that exist today. Vetoing is a yes/no process, so the nature of the process could prevent such political organizations from forming for collectively vetoing or not vetoing legislation.

2.1.4. Legislatures Must Read The Bills They Are Voting On

For every x number of pages that a legislation document has and every y number of pages that added amendments have, there should be a minimum of d number of days between when it was proposed and when it is passed.

  • The purpose of this regulation is to guarantee that the members of the reason system have enough time to read the legislation and carefully evaluate it before making a decision on it.
    • If this rule is always enforced without exception, the worst case scenario would be that the members of the reason system simply don’t read the legislation like they’re supposed to.
    • But at least it’s guaranteed that they will have enough time to read it like they are doing their jobs. This is still an improvement over the existing government which has no such rule.

6. The Penalties For Breaking The Laws

Anybody who kills a parent (accidentally or unintentionally) should be required to pay child support until the children become legal adults. This includes drunk drivers and people who text while driving, if they accidentally kill a parent.

Pedophiles should be required to pay for the mental health expenses of their victims.

A person who takes the life of another cannot claim a legal right to life, lest it would be contradictory to grant the murderer a legal right to life as well.

6.1. Why Corporal Punishment And Humiliation Are More Practical Punishments Than Making Prisoners Spend Years In Prison

It doesn’t make any sense to punish taxpayers by forcing them to pay money that is used to imprison people who broke the law. Why should a person who is lazy and committed crimes have their existence funded by good laboring people who are actually contributing to society? A better method of punish the lawbreakers without wasting taxpayers’ money.

Instead, I propose that corporal punishment should be used instead of prison sentences. This way, lawbreakers are punished, and taxpayers don’t have to pay the price for keeping the lawbreakers locked up in prison. Physical punishment works in Singapore, and Singapore has some of the lowest per capita crime rates in the world (although race and economic factors undoubtedly help with this too). The people who get punished may be unable to work for a few days while they heal from the injury and are experiencing the pain, but then they have to go back to work, and taxpayers won’t have to subsidize their existence.

Western opposition to corporal punishment is just a cultural thing. In Singapore, there is at least a sizable amount of public support for corporal punishment. And after all, aren’t some crimes deserving of corporal punishment, like rape, massive pyramid schemes, torturing, or severe physical abuse? And if corporal punishment does help deter crime, how could the extent to which it does be proven? Couldn’t Singapore’s lower crime rates also be attributed to its strong economic conditions as well?

People cannot imagine what it’s like to spend 5 years in prison. Especially if it’s often far longer than that, it completely ruins someone’s life. People cannot intuitively understand how bad that is. And once the prisoners finally leave the prison, how are they supposed to get back into the real world and return to a normal lifestyle? They could go back to the outside world, but it would be completely alien to them because everybody would be changed and different. Imprisoning someone for several years is a detriment to everybody in society, including hardworking taxpayers who have to pay for the imprisonment. Some wouldn’t even want to go back out into the real world because they’ve become so familiar with the prison world, hence why people often get re-incarcerated immediately after they leave prison.

Another big factor that could change or possibly improve the effects of physical punishment could be to have it be done publicly. Being caned in the townsquare for everybody to see the person deserving the punishment would be a way to humiliate them, so instead of being a purely psychological force for discouraging/preventing crime, it could also be turned into a social force, possibly even a eugenic force if we consider the following scenario. If someone is seen by the public being punished, then fewer people would work with them, and thus they would make less money, and thus they would have fewer children.

Physical punishment also invokes primal instincts, which could very strongly increase its effectiveness and people’s willingness to avoid it as much as possible. If the brain can be roughly divided into about three parts, then the fear of corporal punishment and strong desire to avoid it works off the most basic structures within the human brain. Both of these proposed punishments are based on incentives, and utilize both psychological and economic principles. Being soft on crime causes crime. And making people spend several years in prison when it’s a very costly and ineffective punishment counts as being soft on crime.

Or alternatively, a prisoner could be offered assisted suicide. Then the taxpayers don’t have to subsidize their existence, and the prisoner then has no further opportunities to pass their genes onto the next generation. In most cases, it would just be better for everybody if people serving multiple life sentences in prison were just sentenced to death instead, but the main reason why this doesn’t happen is due to people’s moral dogmas.

10. Enforcing EPC: Why And How

In order to prevent war, disease, and famine, there must be a maximum population limit enforced for the country’s total number of citizens. In order to enforce that population limit, anybody who wants to have children must acquire a Reproduction License for each child that they want to have. Everybody else who is unauthorized to have (more) children must have mandatory contraception, since that is the only way to ensure that people do not create legally unauthorized offspring.

10.1. The Requirements For Being Granted A Reproduction License

  • The Citizen must be law-abiding and have no criminal record,
  • The Citizen must have a high school education [at least],
  • The Citizen must be legally married to someone who will sign a legal agreement with the government to help the raise the child,
  • One of the couples in the marital agreement must be employed in a full-time job,
  • The Citizen must have paid all their Land Value Tax payments (if any),
  • The Citizen must pay the Reproduction Tax for having a child, which is set according to market rates with respect to the Supply, Demand, and Price of Reproduction Licenses.

10.2. How This System Would Be Enforced

There are various ways to do it. If necessary, a mandatory form of legally-approved birth control can be enforced 24/7, unless the citizen has a reproduction license to authorize a pregnancy.

Anybody who manages to illegally have a child without a reproduction license would have committed a felony. A person who violates the policy will have their embryo aborted, could be sterilized, and/or forced to be on birth control until they can repay society for the incurred costs of supporting the child.

Supervised birth control and regular testing should be required for people on welfare and convicted criminals.

Everybody else who is unauthorized to have (more) children must have mandatory contraception, since that is the only way to ensure that people do not create legally unauthorized offspring.

11. The Education System

There will be a public education system and a voucher system, similar to health insurance in the United States, where healthcare is provided by private companies, but there is a public safety net for everybody else. Parents who don’t want to enroll their children into the public education system would not have to pay taxes to fund it, and would only have to pay for their children’s private education expenses.

This free-market approach would provide private education companies with equal opportunity, but also

There’s a good argument for a government-provided safety net for education. Public education can have a unifying effect on the collective values of society, which helps reduce the variance of the society’s values. In a society, you want to distribute the risks of life among everybody, and that’s why safety nets on a collective level are a good idea.
