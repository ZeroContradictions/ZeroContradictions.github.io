1. Regarding Free Will

1.1. Definitions Of Free Will

Before we can even talk about this topic, we have to define free will. There are at least three types:

  1. Metaphysical Free Will (MFW): Determinism is false (this is the opposite of determinism). This does not exist and can be disproven by proof by contradiction.
  2. Subconscious Epistemic Free Will (SEFW): The ability for you to subconsciously choose something, regardless of whether you can actually do it or not, and also regardless of whether determinism is true or false. Having subconscious choices is the exactly same thing as having needs, desires, or preferences. If SEFW exists, then value also exists. ALL sentient organisms have SEFW.
  3. Conscious Epistemic Free Will (CEFW): The ability for you to consciously choose to do what you want, regardless of whether you can actually do it or not, and also regardless of whether determinism is true or false. CEFW is more complex than SEFW since it requires more than just merely desiring things that would satisfy one’s needs/desires. It also requires understandingly what exactly that you want, and knowing how to get it.
  4. Attainable Free Will (AFW): The two conditions that: 1. you can choose to do as you want, and 2. you are able to do it within your current circumstances. Since being conscious is a requirement in order to be ’capable’ of doing something, Attainable Free Will has to be conscious by definition, and it would be the case that Subconscious Attainable Free Will does not exist. Likewise, Conscious Attainable Free Will is the exact same thing as AFW.

1.5. Summary

The definition of free will that most people think about when answering whether it exists or not is metaphysical free will, the opposite of determinism.

So in the simplest of words, all the different main definitions of free will can be summed up in the following stages:

  • The metaphysically contradictory definition
  • Having subconscious desires
  • Having desires and knowing how to fulfill them
  • Having desires, knowing how to fulfill them, and being able to fulfill them

  • Indoctrinate the children with their beliefs, including religion, politics, and other cults
  • Choose the activities that the children do
  • Choose the children’s names
  • Choose how many siblings the children have, and extend their family histories to the children

5. Some Mathematical Wisdom On Free Will

“Choice” does not have to be arbitrary. In principle, if you somehow knew absolutely everything there was to know about the current state of the world, it is possible that you would be able to predict everyone’s choices in the future with 100% accuracy - but they still remain choices. They may be predetermined on a very fundamental level, but for practical purposes it does not make a lot of difference whether you assume them to be predetermined or not.

Mathematics has a very interesting branch called “chaos theory”. What is studied there are models in which small variations in initial conditions lead to dramatic changes in the outcomes. There is a good reason to believe that the Universe as a whole is fairly chaotic; the “butterfly effect” seems to be quite real.

What this implies is that it is extremely hard, if not altogether impossible, to predict choices people will make in the future. When I go to the store tomorrow, will I buy a bottle of wine or not? So many minor factors may affect this choice that the best you can hope to do in the foreseeable future is to give a very-very rough probabilistic estimate of the chances of me doing that.

Suppose you build a very simple general intelligence AI that has the ability to self-learn and modify its own code. The initial code may be very simple, and you might even be able to memorize it in the extreme case - yet, once you launch the AI, just a few seconds later its code will likely become completely unrecognizable and incomprehensible to you. Even though this AI was specifically pre-programmed by you, its choices are still virtually unpredictable.

To me, it does not matter whether “true” free will exists; I believe that it does not and, furthermore, it cannot logically, but that is not very important. What is important is that people’s will is free in that chaotic sense outlined above. If choices cannot be accurately predicted in practice, then they might as well be considered free in the true, fundamental, sense as far as practical considerations go.

And people certainly should be held accountable for their non-coerced choices. If I murder someone, then I hardly can appeal to determinism to claim that I could not help it and became a victim of some grand mechanism no one has any control over; it was still me murdering someone in peaceful circumstances. It was still me violating their rights.

Although it is practical in most cases to pretend that metaphysical free will exists even though it doesn’t exist in any form at all, this pretending should NOT be done in all cases, especially ones where the problems to be solved require acknowledging the non-existence of MFW, such as resolving abnormalities in neurological structures that cause things like OCD, dementia, etc.

Thought on Statistics: If the universe is deterministic and eternalism is correct, then the purpose of statistics is just to predict the course that determinism will take. Regardless of what happens, everything will be known in the future after the events are over, provided that the information was recorded somehow for future generations to see and decipher.
