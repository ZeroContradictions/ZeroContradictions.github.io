  3. AXIOMS: THE AXIOMS OF CLASSICAL LOGIC Epilogue: How the Axioms of Classical Logic were Created/Derived

    1. THE LAW OF IDENTITY
      1. Metaphysically: “A is A” or “Anything is itself.”
      2. For Propositions: “If a proposition is true, then it is true.”
    2. THE LAW OF THE EXCLUDED MIDDLE:
      1. Metaphysically: “Anything is either A or NOTA.”
      2. For Propositions: “A proposition, such as P, is either true or false.”
    3. THE LAW OF NON-CONTRADICTION:
      1. Metaphysically: “Nothing can be both A and NOTA.”
      2. For Propositions: “A proposition, P, can not be both true and false.”

    EXPLAINED: All of our syllogisms rely on these laws - that any thing is equal to itself, that tautologies must be true, and that contradictions must be false. Classical logic holds that everything has a definite, non-contradictory nature. A metaphysical law of identity would hold that to be perceived or even exist at all it must have a definite, non-contradictory nature. CLASSICAL LOGIC OPERATIONS: Logical Connectives (AND, OR, NOT), Conditional, Biconditional, Modus Ponens, ∀, ∃

    4. Evolution Designed how the Senses Work
      1. Although they give us information about reality, the available information and how it’s presented are first and foremost designed to reach the next stage of evolution.

      3. Likewise, the senses are only designed to give us sufficient information about how reality works so that we can survive long enough to produce and foster offspring.
